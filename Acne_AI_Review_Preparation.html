1. Problem & Objective Understanding

20 Marks — Problem Statement · Objective · Importance

1.1 Problem Statement

Acne vulgaris is the most common skin disorder worldwide, affecting approximately 85% of adolescents aged 12–24 and ~12% of adult women. Despite its prevalence, access to dermatological care remains limited — the average wait time to see a dermatologist in India is 2–6 weeks, and in rural/semi-urban areas, many patients have zero access to any skin specialist.

The consequences of untreated or mismanaged acne include:

  • Permanent scarring — especially with severe cystic/nodular acne if not treated early.
  • Psychological distress — studies show acne correlates with depression, social withdrawal, and reduced self-esteem (Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology).
  • Incorrect self-treatment — over-the-counter products chosen without understanding severity can worsen the condition (e.g., using harsh exfoliants on inflamed cystic acne).

The core problem: There is no widely available, free, instant tool that can (a) assess acne severity from a photo, and (b) provide personalized, actionable treatment recommendations — bridging the gap between generic internet advice and a dermatologist visit.

1.2 Objective of the Project

To design and develop an end-to-end AI-powered web application that:

  1. Captures a facial image via webcam or file upload.
  2. Detects and isolates the face region using Google MediaPipe's face detection model (removes background, clothing, hair that could confuse the classifier).
  3. Classifies acne severity into one of three clinical categories — Mild, Moderate, or Severe — using a fine-tuned EfficientNetV2-S deep learning model.
  4. Quantifies certainty via a Softmax-derived confidence score (0–100%) and flags results below 40% as "Inconclusive" for safety.
  5. Profiles the user through a gender-aware skin quiz (oiliness, sensitivity, pore size, breakout frequency, hydration, + gender-specific questions).
  6. Generates personalized treatment plans combining severity classification with skin profile — including AM/PM routines, key active ingredients, product recommendations, do's/don'ts, and timelines.
  7. Tracks progress over time with historical scan logging and severity trend visualization.

Q: Why only 3 severity classes and not more granular?
A: Clinical grading scales (like GAGS) use 5+ levels, but distinguishing between adjacent mild grades requires training data labeled by certified dermatologists — which is expensive and scarce in public datasets. Three classes (Mild/Moderate/Severe) provide clinically actionable boundaries: Mild = self-care, Moderate = monitor closely, Severe = see a doctor immediately. This 3-class approach maximizes the practical utility of the tool while maintaining achievable accuracy with available data.

Preprocessing Pipeline (Step by Step)

  1. Face Detection & Cropping: Google MediaPipe isolates the face bounding box with 20% padding to include forehead/jaw. If no face is detected, a skin-tone HSV check determines if it's a valid close-up skin photo (threshold: 25% skin-colored pixels).
  2. Resizing: All images resized to 224 × 224 × 3 (RGB) to match EfficientNetV2-S input requirements.
  3. Normalization: Pixel values divided by 255.0 to scale from [0, 255] → [0.0, 1.0].
  4. Tensor Expansion: Single image expanded to batch dimension: (224, 224, 3) → (1, 224, 224, 3).

Q: Why not use SMOTE for oversampling?
A: SMOTE works on tabular feature vectors, not raw images. For image data, the standard approach is data augmentation — creating realistic visual variations (flips, rotations, brightness changes) that the model treats as new training samples. This preserves the spatial structure of the image, unlike SMOTE which would interpolate pixel values and produce unrealistic blurry images.

2.2 Model Architecture — EfficientNetV2-S

What is EfficientNetV2?

EfficientNetV2 is a family of convolutional neural networks developed by Google Brain (Tan & Le, 2021). The "V2" improves upon the original EfficientNet by replacing depthwise separable convolutions with Fused-MBConv blocks in early layers. This fusion dramatically reduces memory access cost and accelerates training by 3–4× compared to EfficientNetV1.

Why EfficientNetV2-S Specifically?

  • "S" = Small variant — 21M parameters is the sweet spot for medical imaging tasks with limited data (avoids overfitting that larger variants like V2-L would cause).
  • ImageNet pretrained — Transfer learning provides rich low-level feature detectors (edges, textures, patterns) pre-trained on 14M images, reducing the need for massive acne-specific datasets.
  • Progressive Resizing — V2 was designed to be trained at lower resolutions first (e.g., 128×128) then scaled up (to 224×224), which improves generalization on small datasets.
  • Speed — Trains 3–4× faster than EfficientNetB4 on Colab's free T4 GPU while achieving higher accuracy.

Our Custom Classification Head

EfficientNetV2-S Backbone (pretrained, 21M params) │ ▼ GlobalAveragePooling2D() ← Collapses spatial dims: (7,7,1280) → (1280) │ ▼ Dense(256, activation='relu') ← Learnable feature mapping │ ▼ Dropout(0.4) ← Regularization: randomly zeroes 40% of neurons │ ▼ Dense(3, activation='softmax') ← Output: [P(Mild), P(Moderate), P(Severe)]

Training Strategy — 2-Phase Transfer Learning

PhaseLayers TrainedLearning RateEpochsOptimizerPurpose
Phase 1 (Feature Extraction)Only custom head (top 4 layers)1e-315AdamWTrain head to map pretrained features to our 3 classes
Phase 2 (Fine-Tuning)Head + top 30% of backbone1e-525AdamW + cosine annealingAdapt deeper CNN filters for acne-specific features

Advanced Augmentation During Training

  • Mixup (α=0.2): Blends two training images and their labels linearly. E.g., 80% Image_A + 20% Image_B → Label = 0.8×Label_A + 0.2×Label_B. Forces the model to learn smoother decision boundaries.
  • CutMix: Cuts a rectangular patch from one image and pastes it onto another. The label is adjusted proportionally to the area of the patch. This teaches the model to classify based on partial information.

Q: Why not use a Vision Transformer (ViT)?
A: Vision Transformers require significantly larger datasets (typically 10M+ images) to outperform CNNs. With our ~12K image dataset, a ViT would severely overfit. EfficientNetV2-S with transfer learning is the optimal choice for small-to-medium medical imaging datasets.
Q: What is the loss function?
A: Categorical Cross-Entropy with class weights. The formula is: L = -Σ wᵢ · yᵢ · log(ŷᵢ) where wᵢ compensates for any residual class imbalance, yᵢ is the true one-hot label, and ŷᵢ is the predicted Softmax probability.

PROCESSING (What happens inside)

  1. Image Reception: Flask reads raw bytes from multipart form → np.frombuffer(bytes, np.uint8) → cv2.imdecode() → BGR→RGB conversion.
  2. Face Detection: MediaPipe processes the RGB image. If a face is detected, it returns a relative bounding box. The face is cropped with 20% padding on all sides. If no face → HSV skin-tone fallback (accepts if >25% of pixels are skin-colored).
  3. Preprocessing: cv2.resize(face, (224, 224)) → pixels / 255.0 → np.expand_dims(img, axis=0).
  4. Inference: model.predict(tensor) → Softmax layer outputs array of 3 probabilities summing to 1.0.
  5. Classification: top_idx = np.argmax(preds[0]) → maps index to label (0=Mild, 1=Moderate, 2=Severe). Confidence = preds[0][top_idx] × 100.
  6. Safety Check: If confidence < 40.0% → severity overridden to "Inconclusive".
  7. Recommendation Lookup: recommendations.json is queried by severity key. Returns routines, ingredients, products, do's/don'ts, timeline, and acne type guesses.

Q: How exactly is the confidence score calculated?
A: The final layer of EfficientNetV2-S uses a Softmax activation function. Softmax converts raw logits into a probability distribution where all values sum to 1.0. The confidence is simply the maximum probability multiplied by 100. For example, if the output is [0.05, 0.87, 0.08], the predicted class is "Moderate" (index 1) with 87% confidence. Exact code: confidence = float(preds[0][np.argmax(preds[0])]) * 100.

4.2 Best Performing Model

EfficientNetV2-S with 2-phase transfer learning (frozen → fine-tuned) and Mixup/CutMix augmentation achieved the highest validation accuracy (~90%+), outperforming VGG-16 (78%), ResNet-50 (82%), and EfficientNetB4 (85%) on the same dataset and split.

4.3 Are Results Convincing?

✅ Yes, for preliminary screening: The model reliably distinguishes between mild and severe cases — the most clinically important boundary. If severe acne is flagged, the user is directed to seek professional help immediately. The confidence score provides full transparency — users can see exactly how confident the AI is.
⚠️ Honest Caveats: The model is trained on curated internet datasets that may not represent all skin tones, lighting conditions, or camera qualities in real-world use. Performance may degrade on Fitzpatrick Scale V–VI skin tones. This is precisely why: (1) we implemented the 40% safety threshold, (2) every results page includes a medical disclaimer, and (3) severe cases are escalated to professional care.

4.4 Limitations of the System

  1. Dataset Bias: Training data may underrepresent darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick V–VI), potentially reducing accuracy for those demographics. Curated datasets skew toward lighter skin tones common in Western dermatology textbooks.
  2. 3-Class Granularity: Only Mild/Moderate/Severe. Clinical dermatology uses finer scales (GAGS: 0–44 point scoring) that require expert-labeled data our public datasets don't provide.
  3. No Lesion Localization: The model classifies the overall image severity — it does not detect, count, or pinpoint individual pimples, cysts, or nodules. Adding YOLO-based object detection would address this.
  4. Lighting & Camera Dependency: Poor lighting, motion blur, or very low-resolution cameras degrade confidence significantly. The system relies on users providing reasonable photo quality.
  5. Static Recommendations: The treatment engine is rule-based (JSON lookup), not a continuously learning system. It doesn't adapt based on user feedback or treatment outcomes.
  6. Not a Medical Device: Cannot replace professional diagnosis. Intended as a screening/educational aid only — clearly disclosed via on-screen disclaimer.
Q: What happens if someone uploads a non-face image (e.g., a landscape)?
A: The system has a 2-level rejection mechanism: (1) MediaPipe checks for a face — if none is found, (2) an HSV skin-tone analysis checks if ≥25% of pixels are skin-colored (for close-up acne photos). If both checks fail, the API returns {"status": "error", "message": "No face detected. Please upload a clear photo of your face."} and the frontend shows a clear error message.
Q: Could the model give a wrong prediction with high confidence?
A: Yes — this is a known limitation of Softmax-based classifiers (overconfidence). Softmax always outputs probabilities summing to 1.0 even on out-of-distribution inputs. Mitigation strategies include: (1) our 40% threshold, (2) calibration techniques like temperature scaling, and (3) future use of Monte Carlo Dropout for uncertainty estimation.

5. Learning, Innovation & Presentation

20 Marks — Improvements · Real-World Apps · Communication

5.1 Ideas for Improvement / Extension

  1. Multi-Label Classification: Detect specific acne types (whiteheads, blackheads, cysts, nodules, papules) simultaneously using sigmoid outputs instead of softmax, allowing an image to be tagged with multiple types at once.
  2. Lesion Detection with YOLOv8: Add object detection to count and localize individual acne lesions on a face map, providing a quantitative "lesion count" metric.
  3. Diverse Dataset (Fitzpatrick Scale I–VI): Partner with dermatology departments to collect and label images across all skin tones, reducing racial bias.
  4. Temporal Before/After Analysis: Use structural similarity (SSIM) or feature-based comparison to quantitatively measure improvement between two photos taken weeks apart.
  5. Explainable AI (Grad-CAM): Generate heatmaps showing which regions of the face the model focused on when making its prediction, increasing user trust and debugging capability.
  6. Telemedicine Integration: Allow users to share their AI report directly with a registered dermatologist for remote consultation — monetizable as a premium feature.
  7. Mobile App (React Native): Port the web application to native iOS/Android for push notifications (routine reminders) and offline analysis.
  8. Federated Learning: Allow the model to improve from user data without centralizing sensitive facial images — privacy-preserving continuous learning.
